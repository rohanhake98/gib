### Executive Summary (1–2 pages)

The Great Indian Bustard (*Ardeotis nigriceps*), one of the heaviest flying birds in the world (up to 15–18 kg, ~1 m tall, wingspan 210–250 cm), is **Critically Endangered (IUCN 3.1)**. Once widespread across dry grasslands and shrublands of the Indian subcontinent (historically in 11 states plus parts of Pakistan), it has lost ~90% of its range.

**Population Trajectory (Key Data Points):**
- **1969**: ~1,260 individuals (Dharmakumarsinhji).
- **1978**: ~745
- **2001**: ~600
- **2008**: ~300 (or fewer than 250 mature individuals)
- **2011–2018**: Declined to ~150 or fewer; extirpated from much of former range.
- **~2025**: Signs of stabilization/recovery. Wild population estimates vary (e.g., ~130 ±21 in key Jaisalmer areas per WII 2025 census; total wild ~100–150+). Captive breeding has boosted totals: ~45–82 in breeding centers by early 2026, with new chicks via natural mating and artificial insemination. Combined figures in some reports reach ~173–198+ (including captive and specific sites).

**Recent Positive Developments (2024–2026):**
- Captive breeding program (launched ~2019, ramped up under Project GIB) at centers like Sudasari and Ramdevra (Rajasthan) has produced dozens of chicks. In 2025: ~25 chicks; early 2026: additional chicks reported. Captive count reached 70–82+.
- “Jumpstart” initiative: Successful translocation of a fertile egg from Rajasthan to a wild female’s nest in Gujarat’s Kutch (Naliya), hatching in March 2026 after ~10 years without local breeding success.
- Community involvement (e.g., Bishnoi community) and habitat protection in Desert National Park (DNP), Thar Desert.

**Primary Threats:** Habitat loss/fragmentation (agriculture, infrastructure, mining), power line collisions (major adult mortality source due to poor frontal vision and heavy body), poaching/hunting, disturbance, predation on eggs/chicks, and low reproductive rate (usually 1 egg per season, slow maturity).

**Outlook:** Intensive interventions have slowed the decline and shown early recovery signals, but the species remains on the brink. Long-term success depends on scaling habitat restoration, power line mitigation (e.g., undergrounding/marking), community stewardship, and safe reintroduction from 2026 onward. Population viability analyses indicate small populations (<35–50) are highly vulnerable without aggressive action.

The report synthesizes historical data, recent censuses, threats analysis, conservation measures, and recommendations, supported by graphs, tables, and visuals.

### 1. Introduction and Taxonomy (1–2 pages)

**Scientific Classification:**
- Kingdom: Animalia
- Phylum: Chordata
- Class: Aves
- Order: Otidiformes
- Family: Otididae
- Genus: *Ardeotis*
- Species: *Ardeotis nigriceps* (Vigors, 1831)

**Common Names:** Great Indian Bustard (GIB), Godawan (Rajasthan), Ghorad (Gujarat).

**Physical Description:** Large, ground-dwelling bird with cryptic brown plumage, black cap (males more contrasting), white underparts/neck in males. Males display inflatable neck sacs during breeding. Prefers open arid/semi-arid grasslands with scattered shrubs for foraging (insects, seeds, small vertebrates) and nesting on ground.

**Historical Distribution:** Widespread in northwestern and west-central India; also Pakistan. Now restricted primarily to Rajasthan (core: Thar Desert, DNP, Jaisalmer), with small/fragmented populations in Gujarat (Kutch), Maharashtra, and traces in Madhya Pradesh, Andhra Pradesh, Karnataka.

**Ecological Role:** Indicator species for healthy grassland ecosystems; helps control insect populations.

**Legal Status:**
- IUCN: Critically Endangered (since 2011; criteria A2a+4acd; C1).
- Indian Wildlife (Protection) Act, 1972: Schedule I.
- CITES: Appendix I.
- CMS: Relevant appendices.
- State bird of Rajasthan.

### 3. Habitat and Distribution (2 pages)

**Preferred Habitat:** Open dry grasslands, scrublands, semi-arid plains. Avoids dense vegetation or heavily cultivated areas. Relies on insects and seeds; needs large, undisturbed patches for lekking (display) and nesting.

**Current Strongholds:** Desert National Park and surrounding Thar landscapes in Rajasthan. Fragmented pockets elsewhere.

**Habitat Loss Quantification:** ~90% range loss due to conversion to agriculture (sorghum/millet to sugarcane/cotton), irrigation, roads, mining, and renewable energy infrastructure (wind/solar with associated power lines).

### 4. Threats Analysis (4–5 pages)

**Major Threats (Ranked by Impact):**
1. **Power Line Collisions/Electrocution:** Leading cause of adult mortality. GIBs have limited frontal vision and fly low/heavy; lines cross prime habitat.
2. **Habitat Loss and Fragmentation:** Agricultural expansion, infrastructure, overgrazing, mining.
3. **Hunting and Poaching:** Historical sport/meat hunting; persists in some areas (e.g., Pakistan border regions).
4. **Disturbance and Predation:** Free-ranging dogs, livestock trampling eggs, human activity.
5. **Low Reproductive Success:** One egg/clutch, high chick/egg mortality, delayed maturity.
6. **Other:** Pesticides, vehicle collisions, climate impacts on grasslands.

**Quantitative Insights:** Power lines identified as a key driver in multiple studies; PVA (Population Viability Analysis) by Dutta et al. (2010) highlighted extinction risk without intervention.

### 7. Discussion, Challenges, and Recommendations (3–4 pages)

**Challenges:** Small effective population size (genetic concerns), slow reproduction, vast monitoring areas, balancing renewable energy development with biodiversity, funding sustainability, climate change effects on arid grasslands.

**Recommendations:**
- Scale power line mitigation nationwide in bustard habitats.
- Expand protected grassland corridors and restoration.
- Accelerate safe reintroduction protocols (2026+).
- Strengthen community incentives and monitoring networks.
- Genetic studies and PVA updates.
- International collaboration (e.g., via CMS, IUCN SSC Bustard Specialist Group).
- Integrate with broader grassland/sustainable development policies.

**Future Outlook:** With continued momentum, stabilization is possible, but extinction risk remains high without sustained, science-based action. Early 2025–2026 breeding successes provide cautious optimism.
