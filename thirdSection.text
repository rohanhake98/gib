Historical and Current Distribution of the Great Indian Bustard (Ardeotis nigriceps)The Great Indian Bustard (GIB), a large ground-dwelling bird endemic to the Indian subcontinent, has undergone one of the most dramatic range contractions among avian species in India. Once widespread across extensive dry grasslands, semi-arid plains, and scrublands, its distribution has shrunk to fragmented pockets, primarily in Rajasthan, with tiny, often non-viable populations elsewhere. This section outlines the historical range, the drivers and timeline of collapse, and the current (as of recent surveys up to ~2025–early 2026) distribution, setting the context for the multi-decadal demographic analysis and the 2026 “Jumpstart” recovery milestone. 

en.wikipedia.org

Historical DistributionHistorically, the GIB was distributed across much of western and central India, as well as parts of Pakistan. It occurred in at least 11 Indian states: Punjab, Haryana, Uttar Pradesh, Madhya Pradesh, Chhattisgarh, Odisha (formerly Orissa), Andhra Pradesh, Rajasthan, Gujarat, Maharashtra, Karnataka, and Tamil Nadu. Its strongholds were the Thar Desert in the northwest (especially Rajasthan) and the Deccan tableland/plateau in the peninsula. 

datazone.birdlife.org

The species favored flat, open landscapes with minimal visual obstruction—dry grasslands, scrublands, and semi-arid areas where it could forage on insects, seeds (particularly from sorghum and millet), and small vertebrates. In the 19th and early 20th centuries, it was relatively common in suitable habitats across these regions. A 1969 estimate placed the population at around 1,260 individuals across these states. 

india.mongabay.com

Archaeofaunal records and older surveys (e.g., Manakadan 1990; Rahmani & Manakadan 1990) confirm its broader past presence, including summer visitors or occasional records in Pakistan. The bird’s large home ranges (often averaging ~100 km² or more) allowed it to move opportunistically across landscapes following resource availability. 

currentconservation.org

By the late 20th century, however, habitat loss, conversion of grasslands to agriculture (especially irrigation-intensive crops like sugarcane and cotton), mechanized farming, mining, road-building, power lines, and hunting/poaching had already begun fragmenting populations. It was extirpated from many areas, including states like Haryana, Punjab, Odisha, Uttar Pradesh, Tamil Nadu, and later Madhya Pradesh. Approximately 90% of its former geographic range has been lost or severely degraded. 

britannica.com

Multi-Decadal Decline and FragmentationPopulation estimates illustrate the collapse:~1,260 birds in 1969.
Declines to hundreds by the 1970s–2000s (e.g., ~745 in 1978, ~600 in 2001, ~300 in 2008).
Further drop to critically low levels by the 2010s, with the global population estimated at ~150 or fewer mature individuals in some recent assessments (though captive breeding and surveys show fluctuations). 

india.mongabay.com

The species was listed as Endangered by IUCN in 1994 and uplisted to Critically Endangered in 2011. Eight or so fragmented populations persisted for a time in Rajasthan (shared border with Pakistan), Gujarat, Maharashtra, Andhra Pradesh, Karnataka, and (formerly) Madhya Pradesh. The largest concentration has consistently been in Rajasthan’s Thar Desert, particularly districts like Jaisalmer, Barmer, and Bikaner, with smaller groups in areas such as Kachchh (Gujarat), Warora (Maharashtra), and Bellary (Karnataka). 

datazone.birdlife.org

Key threats driving the distributional collapse include:Habitat loss and degradation: Conversion of grasslands to croplands, overgrazing, and infrastructure (solar/wind farms, power lines that cause collisions).
Anthropogenic pressures: Hunting, egg collection, disturbance, and predation by feral dogs.
Low reproductive rate: The species has low fecundity, making recovery slow even under protection.
Fragmentation: Remaining birds occupy isolated pockets, reducing gene flow and breeding success.

By the 2010s–2020s, the species was considered on the precipice, with most of the global population (>90% in some estimates) restricted to Rajasthan’s Thar landscape, and tiny, at-risk groups elsewhere (often <20 birds per state, with some nearing local extinction). Breeding had become limited to specific pockets in Jaisalmer, and sightings/breeding in states like Gujarat became rare (e.g., no confirmed breeding for years in some areas). 

currentconservation.org

Current Distribution (Mid-2020s Context)As of recent surveys and reports (circa 2014–2025 data, with some updates into 2026), the GIB is principally confined to Rajasthan, with very small populations in Gujarat, Maharashtra, Karnataka, and Andhra Pradesh. It has been declared absent or nearly so from Madhya Pradesh and several other former range states.Rajasthan (primary stronghold): Holds the vast majority (~100–150 birds in various estimates), concentrated in the Thar Desert, especially Desert National Park (DNP) and surrounding areas in Jaisalmer, Barmer, and Bikaner districts. Breeding is largely limited to two main pockets in western Jaisalmer. Densities are low (e.g., ~0.61 birds/100 km² in sampled Thar areas). Telemetry shows fragmented movements compared to historical patterns. 

datazone.birdlife.org

Gujarat: Small population (estimates ~5–20 birds, mainly in Kachchh/Naliya area). Breeding had been absent for about a decade in some reports until recent conservation interventions.
Maharashtra: Very small numbers (~2–10 birds, e.g., Vidarbha/Warora region); some surveys failed to detect birds in former sites. At high risk of local extinction.
Karnataka and Andhra Pradesh: Tiny, non-viable populations (often estimated at <10–15 combined); considered close to disappearance in Karnataka.
Pakistan: Occasional reports, but breeding details are limited and populations are not considered significant.

Overall, the species now occupies a tiny fraction of its historical range, with habitat preferences still centered on open, undisturbed grasslands and scrub. Total wild population estimates have hovered around 100–150 birds in recent years, though exact figures vary with survey methods and inclusion of juveniles. Captive breeding programs (e.g., in Rajasthan) have increased numbers in controlled settings, reaching dozens of individuals. 

bnhs.org

The distribution remains highly fragmented, with birds in isolated pockets vulnerable to stochastic events, infrastructure development (e.g., renewable energy projects in the Thar), and ongoing habitat pressures.Link to the 2026 “Jumpstart” Recovery MilestoneAgainst this backdrop of demographic collapse, the 2026 “Jumpstart” initiative represents a targeted intervention. In Gujarat’s Kutch region, conservation teams used an innovative “Jumpstart” approach—transporting a viable egg from Rajasthan and fostering it with a wild female—to achieve the first confirmed chick hatching in nearly a decade. This cross-state effort, coordinated with broader Project Great Indian Bustard initiatives (including captive breeding centers in Rajasthan producing dozens of chicks), signals an attempt to bolster small peripheral populations and restore breeding in former range areas. While the overall wild population remains critically low, such milestones, alongside habitat protection in the Thar and efforts to mitigate power-line collisions and land-use changes, offer cautious hope for stabilizing distribution and preventing further local extinctions. 

openthemagazine.com

In summary, the GIB’s historical distribution spanned vast semi-arid landscapes across 11+ Indian states and Pakistan, supporting over a thousand individuals. Today, it is reduced to a few fragmented strongholds, overwhelmingly in Rajasthan’s Thar Desert, with marginal populations elsewhere that are often not self-sustaining. This contraction—driven by anthropogenic pressures over decades—underpins the urgency of the multi-decadal analysis and targeted recovery actions like the 2026 Jumpstart milestone.
